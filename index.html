Genetic Cancer Research

Can I use gene expression levels to predict whether or not cancer patients will relapse?

Find Out

Background

Cancer develops from genetic mutations that prevent programmed cell deaths. These cells keep growing and dividing and stealing resources from normally functioning cells. New technologies allow us to quickly quantify the gene expression levels in cells and we can use this information to target specific genes for cancer therapy.

In this project, I analyze the expression levels from 22,215 genes taken from 424 ER+ type breast cancer patients who were treated with surgery or surgery and radiation, but not treated with systemic hormonal therapy or chemotherapy. These patients fall into 1 of 2 categories. NoRelapse patients did not relapse within the 6.5 year study they participated in, while Relapse patients did relapse within the 6.5 year study. I split the patients into a 212-patient training set (on which I build my 3 models) and a 212-patient testing set. The training set has 152 NoRelapse patients and 60 Relapse patients. The testing set has 137 NoRelapse patients and 75 Relapse patients.

The goals of my analysis are: 1. to classify patients into either the NoRelapse category or the Relapse category solely based on their gene expression profiles, and 2. to identify the genes that separate between the two classes the best.

Understand the Data

The Data

DNA Microarray

The gene expression levels come from DNA microarrays. A DNA microarray is a collection of DNA spots attached to a solid surface which scientists can then use to measure the expression levels of large numbers of genes simulataneously.

Gene Expression

The process by which information from a gene is expressed as a physical feature. Measuring gene expression gives us the ability to quantify the level at which a particular gene is expressed within a cell, tissue, or organism.

Lab to Lab Variation

These data were collected from 3 different European studies. Microarray data collected on the same phenotypes but by different labs can be surprisingly different, and results obtained from one dataset often do not generalize to the others.

High Dimensionality

The data are very high-dimensional meaning there are a lot of features (22,215 genes) and few samples (424 patients). This makes it difficult to understand the true underlying relationships between genes and motivates the algorithm I describe below.

Methods

I compared 3 different machine learning techniques for my analysis. The first two are standard classifiers: a Random Forest Classifier and a Gradient Boosting Classifier. The third classifier is one that I read about in a paper then built from scratch. I describe the algorithm in the next section.

What is TSP?

Top Scoring Pair(s) Classifier

I wanted to use a highly transparent, easily interpretable method where I would be able to advise scientists on which genes to focus on when researching ER+ breast cancer development, so I built the algorithm discussed in the linked paper. It is a comparison-based classification technique which chooses a gene pair with the largest score relative to a measurement of discrimination. The pair with the largest score (the TSP) votes for the class which makes the observed ordering within that pair the most likely. There are no parameters to tune.

Note: If you don't want to read about how the algorithm works, you can skip the rest of this section.

The algorithm works as follows. First, suppose I have n patients, each of whom have G genes whose expression levels are measured using DNA microarrays. Each patient either did not relapse (0) or did relapse (1). I choose a pair of genes i and j. I call this pair X. X will be a nx2 matrix with n patients and 2 genes in the pair. Next, for each patient in X, I rank the expression levels. I call this nx2 rank matrix R where each element in the matrix is either a 0 or a 1 depending on which gene in the pair had the larger expression level. I repeat this process for every gene pair.

I want to find the gene pairs for which there is a significant difference in the probability of gene i < gene j from class 0 to class 1. The probability is esimated by the relative frequencies of occurrences of gene i < gene j. Thus, I calculate 2 probabilities fo each gene pair: 1) probability that gene i is expressed less than gene j given class 0 and 2) the probability that gene i is expressed less than gene j given class 1. The larger the difference in these probabilities, the more discriminatory the gene pair. Since I want to find the most discriminatory gene pairs, I calculate this difference for every gene pair and denote it as the score of gene pair (i, j). The pair with the largest score is the pair I will base my classification on.

Results

There are several metrics I look at to determine how well my models performed. The most basic one is accuracy which calculates correct classifications / total classifications. Since my testing set had 137 NoRelapse patients of 212 patients, if I predict that everyone in my testing set was NoRelapse, I'd get 137/212 = 65% accuracy. This is my "null accuracy" that my models will to to improve upon.

Another important metric to look at is the True Positive Rate vs. the False Positive Rate. The TPR is the percentage of time I correctly classify a patient as Relapse and the FPR is the percentage of time I incorrectly classify a patient as Relapse (meaning the patient was in reality NoRelapse). I want to maximize the TPR and minimize the FPR because of course, I don't want to give a patient a new treatment if she did not relapse.
